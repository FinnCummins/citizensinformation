Mental health services for children and young people in Ireland

Introduction

It can be an overwhelming experience for a parent when your child is experiencing difficulties with their mental health. Sometimes, mental health issues can be eased by getting support from family or friends. Other times, mental health issues can be more complex and difficult to deal with.

There are supports and resources that are freely available to everyone that are intended to help people to manage ‘mild’ mental health challenges and protect their mental health and wellbeing. These include websites like spunout.ie and yourmentalhealth.ie . It also includes helplines and advocacy services funded and supported by the HSE. For example, 50808 , Childline , and Samaritans .

When more support is needed, there are mental health services specifically for children. The service or organisation you go to depends on what your child is going through is considered mild, or more severe in nature. Even when a mental health difficulty is categorised as ‘mild’, that does not mean it is not serious or important. Any type or level of mental health difficulties can still be distressing and have a significant impact.

Knowing what mental health services there are and how to access them isn’t always easy. Here we describe the main services and how they can help.

Your GP

The first step to knowing where to go is to visit your GP.

You can visit your GP along with your young person. The HSE has a list of questions you, or your child, might find useful when you are talking to the GP or other healthcare professional.

Explain what you’re worried about and any changes, however big or small, you have noticed in the last weeks or months. Your GP is likely to ask lots of questions. It is important to tell the GP everything you are concerned about so can then either advise what to do next or give you a referral for the right services, if appropriate.

Your GP may want to meet with your young person alone for a period. That’s normal.

Your GP will take a number of factors into account to identify the right support or service for your child, including their age, the type of symptoms they are experiencing, the severity of their symptoms and their level of risk.

Primary care services

The next level of supports and services are sometimes described as primary care services. These are designed for people who are going through ‘mild to moderate’ mental health difficulties. The types of support offered at this level tend to be brief and last for a specified time period.

  • Jigsaw offers free one-to-one mental health support for young people aged 12-25 with mild to moderate mental health concerns. Referrals for young people under 18 are accepted from parents and health care professionals. You can email or phone your local Jigsaw service to ask for an appointment.
  • HSE Primary Care Psychology services are based in local primary care centres all over Ireland. They provide a broad range of free services to children aged 0 - 17 years of age. Each primary care centre usually has a team of occupational therapists, psychologists, nurses and social workers. Services include individual therapy, group-based therapeutic work for children, adolescents and parents, and various types of assessments. Referrals are accepted from parents and healthcare professionals.
  • Family Resource Centres run by the Child and Family Agency Tusla around the country deliver free community-based supports to children and parents, and some services have access to counselling.

Specialist services

The next level of supports and services are designed for people who are experiencing moderate to severe mental health issues. The services at this level are sometimes described as specialist services. They are usually led by psychiatrists, and most of the people who are accessing supports at this level will have a clinical mental health diagnosis. To access these supports and services your child will need a referral from your GP or healthcare professional.

CAMHS

The main specialist mental health support for young people under the age of 18 is child and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS). CAMHS is designed for young people with moderate to severe mental health difficulties. CAMHS prioritises referrals on the basis of risk, which means that people who are at considered at highest risk will be seen sooner, and those categorised as lower risk will have to wait longer to be seen. CAHMS assesses and treats conditions like attention deficit hyperactivity disorder and moderate to severe depression, anxiety, eating disorders, and self-harm.

CAMHS have three levels of referrals:

  • Emergency referral is when there is a significant risk to the young person’s safety, such as suicide attempt, significant self-harm or a severe eating disorder. These referrals will be prioritised and seen quickly.
  • Urgent referral is where there are high concerns for the young person but it is not a crisis or emergency.
  • Referrals are categorised as routine when there is no immediate risk.

Specialist services are also for people who are experiencing severe and complex mental health issues that need intensive support. This can involve a stay in a hospital or a similar supported living setting where a team of mental health professionals can monitor symptoms and give specialist support and treatments.

Inpatient treatment can be really helpful if your child's symptoms are so severe that they are making it difficult for them to function, if their safety is at risk, or if they also need some physical health treatment because of their mental health issues.

Private mental health care services

There are mental health professionals in private practice all over Ireland that provide therapy sessions for young people who are under 18. It is important to check what experience they have with working with young people, the type of support they offer, and the cost before making an appointment. You do not need a referral from your GP, you can just make arrangements directly.

Not all therapists are trained in the same way or have the same qualifications. Spunout has information to help you understand the difference between counsellors and psychotherapists and the types of therapy available.

Non-profit organisations

Some charities and organisations offer services you might find useful when looking for support for your young person. Some of these organisations receive HSE funding.

  • Bodywhys (Eating Disorders Association of Ireland)
  • Childline
  • Teenline
  • Barnardos
  • Pieta House

Get urgent help

Call 999 or 112 if you or someone you know is about to harm themselves or someone else.
